Agrobiodiversity in a Oaxacan Market: A Window into Bio-Cultural Diversity

In the summer of 2023, I participated in an overseas Stanford program in Oaxaca, Mexico focused on bio-cultural diversity and community-based conservation. Led by Dr. Rodolfo Dirzo, this program explored how Indigenous communities and local ecosystems in Oaxaca shape one another, as well as the deep connections between their cultural traditions and environmental stewardship.

We conducted a study on agricultural biodiversity, or agrobiodiversity, at Oaxaca's Central de Abastos, the large central market of Oaxaca City. This central market is a vibrant hub of culture and commerce—alive with the calls of hawkers, colorful sights of fresh flowers and produce, and smells of wood smoke and grilled meats. Our study revealed the incredible variety of crops and wild foods sold there, as well as their sources and native ranges.

Of the sampled market goods, most were sourced within Mexico, with only a small fraction imported. Although just 35% of the market items came from Oaxaca, these items alone accounted for 65% of the species diversity. Oaxaca's outsized contribution to produce diversity is a testament to its strong local food systems, which are largely supported by small and family-owned farms that operate independently or through cooperatives.

In Mexico, Oaxaca is the leading contributor of agrobiodiversity to the market, bringing 155 distinct produce varieties. The state's agriculture diversity stems from its complex geography of mountain ranges, valleys, and varied microclimates that support a wide array of ecosystems. For thousands of years, Indigenous communities have cultivated these lands, domesticating wild plants and passing down sustainable farming practices still used today.

Of all the market items, chile peppers stood out as the most biodiverse produce with over 60 varieties. Long before it spread to cuisines worldwide, the chile pepper was first domesticated by Indigenous farmers in what is now central-east Mexico—a region stretching from southern Puebla and northern Oaxaca to southern Veracruz. This ancient fruit was held sacred for thousands of years by the Inca, Aztec, and Maya civilizations, who used them in rituals, divination, and medicinal practices. Today, the chile pepper remains a cornerstone of Mexican cuisine, with dozens of varieties endemic to Oaxaca, including chilhuacle, chile de agua, chile tabiche, and more.
